FE Modelling
“One of the big problems facing engineers is that we can solve only the simplest of
structures by hand because the mathematical process rapidly becomes too complex.
This means that we need techniques such as FEA working on computers to solve them
for us. Our job is to produce good answers, and we do that by putting good data into
appropriate software. But how do we know what is good data and good software?” [2]

Learning outcomes:
• Understand the importance of implementing an effective structural Finite
  Element modelling process and its fundamental principles

                    This is not a fundamental FE theory course.
However, you need some understanding of the theory to appreciate the importance of
your modelling choices.

How it works
• FE model solves a boundary value problem – solves differential equations (PDEs)
  within a specific domain

• Typical ingredients in the context of physical systems:
    1. Domain with a boundary
    2. Kinematics: a field of interest what are we solving for i.e. displacement,
       velocities, temperature etc.
    3. Equilibrium/balance principles: laws of physics (i.e. force balance) - Equation
       we want to solve
   4. Constitutive equations: How the kinematics relates to the equilibrium (material
      response)
   5. Boundary conditions

Element types
1D element

• Member is slender: length is much larger than the cross-section dimensions → the
  members behavior can be represented along its centerline; field variable varies in
  one-dimension only.
• The stress variation within the cross-section is not explicitly solved
• Cross-section properties have to be defined for all elements.

Modelling principles

1. Use the simplest practical model
2. Estimate results before you run the analysis
3. Increment the complexity
4. When you get a result, assume that they may have errors
5. Troubleshoot errors

\section{Deliverable 2: Material, Section, and Loading Data }
\subsection{Material and Section Properties and Loading Data}

The global FE model was defined using linear-elastic steel. In accordance with the brief, all members were assigned an elastic modulus of \SI{200}{\giga\pascal}. A Poisson's ratio of 0.30 and density of \SI{7850}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed} were adopted so that self-weight could be included automatically in GSA. S355 steel was used for the primary roof members, which is consistent with the Heathrow T5 project information. The high tie was modelled with an initial prestress of \SI{450}{\mega\pascal}.

Only vertical symmetric loading was considered. The characteristic permanent roof load was taken as the sum of the aluminium standing seam roof (\SI{0.10}{\kilo\pascal}), structural deck (\SI{0.15}{\kilo\pascal}), insulation/acoustic layers (\SI{0.40}{\kilo\pascal}) and the service allowance (\SI{0.15}{\kilo\pascal}), giving $G_k = \SI{0.80}{\kilo\pascal}$. The live load imposed on the roof was taken as $Q_k = \SI{0.60}{\kilo\pascal}$. Therefore, the governing vertical ultimate limit state (ULS) combination used in the model was
\[
1.35G_k + 1.5Q_k = 1.35(0.80) + 1.5(0.60) = \SI{1.98}{\kilo\pascal}.
\]
Using a width of \SI{36}{\metre} for one transverse frame, it is found that a line load of \SI{71.28}{\kilo\newton\per\metre} is applied in addition to the self-weight of the primary members.

The strap members were modelled as rod elements. Therefore, only their axial stiffness was represented in the global model, using an equivalent steel area of $A = 2 \times 500 \times 40 = \SI{40000}{\milli\metre\squared}$. The spacing between the two plates was neglected in this idealisation because the rod elements do not carry bending.

\section{Deliverable 3: Initial Model Validation}
\subsection{Assumptions}

One important modelling assumption is that the real roof has been reduced from a 3D structural system to a 2D frame idealisation. This means only the in-plane behaviour of one transverse frame is represented directly, while out-of-plane bending, torsion and any load-sharing between adjacent frames are neglected. As a result, the model cannot capture twisting of the curved rafters, weak-axis bending caused by geometric eccentricity, or other local 3D effects associated with the actual connection arrangement. This simplification is acceptable for an initial global study because the loading considered here is vertical and symmetric, so the dominant response is expected to remain in-plane, but it should still be recognised as a source of modelling error when interpreting the validation results.

\section{Deliverable 4: Mesh Refinement Sensitivity Study \& Model Verification}

\subsection{Introduction to Discretisation Error}
Finite element analysis provides an approximation, not the exact analytical solution. When dividing a continuous structure into finite elements, discretisation error is introduced from two main sources: element behaviour error (dependent on the chosen shape functions) and mesh density error (dependent on element sizing). Mesh refinement evaluates whether this approximation is acceptable by increasing the degrees of freedom (DoF) until the results converge. To isolate the mesh density error without distorting the global physical behaviour, refinement was strictly limited to the continuous curve of the arch rafter. Based on St-Venant's principle, the detailed stress distribution near load applications or complex joints only matters locally. Refining the complex intersections at the tusk rafters, legs, and high ties could artificially induce stress singularities due to idealised geometry (such as re-entrant corners), which do not converge. Therefore, those boundary members remained constant while the arch was discretised into varying densities.

\subsection{Convergence Study Methodology}
Following standard FEA procedures, the convergence study was conducted as follows:

To evaluate convergence, it is necessary to check global quantities away from boundary singularities. Maximum vertical crown deflection ($\delta_z$) and maximum arch bending moment ($M_y$) were selected. Peak local stresses were ignored as they are highly dependent on constraint application and may not converge.

Three mesh densities were evaluated to test the sensitivity of the global response to geometric discretisation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mesh 1 (Coarsest):} Highly simplified faceted geometry merging multiple coordinate segments.
    \item \textbf{Mesh 2 (Coarse):} Partially merged geometry.
    \item \textbf{Mesh 3 (Baseline):} The exact original coordinate nodes defined in the project data.
\end{itemize}

A solution is considered converged when further refinement causes negligible change in the target quantity. The following strict convergence criterion was applied between iterations:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{|\delta_{n} - \delta_{n-1}|}{\delta_{n}} < 2\%
\end{equation}

As demonstrated in Table \ref{tab:mesh_sensitivity}, transitioning from the coarsest faceted model to the baseline geometry produced a noticeable change in both deflection and internal force, showing that the coarser meshes artificially stiffened the arch. The smaller change between Mesh 2 and Mesh 3 indicates that the solution is approaching convergence; the final percentage change should be inserted once calculated explicitly.

\subsection{Model Verification}
Because a converged answer to an incorrect model is still wrong, the following basic verification checks were successfully performed on the converged Baseline model to ensure the solution process was correctly implemented:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Error Warnings:} The GSA solver completed the static analysis with no warnings regarding matrix singularities or instabilities.
    \item \textbf{Overall Equilibrium Check:} The sum of the vertical reaction forces ($R_z$) at the pinned supports was confirmed to equal the total applied vertical gravity loads ($\sum F = R \approx 0$).
    \item \textbf{Symmetry:} Because the structural geometry and the critical ULS load combination are perfectly symmetric, the resulting internal forces and deflected shape were verified to be entirely symmetric.
    \item \textbf{Qualitative Check:} The deflected shape aligns with physical expectations; the vertical loads cause the arch crown to displace downwards, putting the main ties into pure axial tension.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Conclusion}
Based on the achieved convergence and successful model verification, Mesh 3 is deemed structurally sufficient. It provides an accurate global approximation without the unnecessary computational expense of higher-order refinement, and has been adopted for the remainder of this report.

\section{Deliverable 5: Influence of Prestressing Assumptions }
The influence of prestressing assumptions on the global structural response of the roof system was investigated by comparing two modelling approaches. In the simplified coursework model, prestressing of the high tie is assumed to occur after the complete erection of the roof structure. In contrast, the supplementary documentation describing the actual construction process indicates that the central arch section was assembled at low level and the high tie cables were tensioned during erection prior to the final load transfer.

To assess the impact of this assumption, two simplified models were considered. The first model applies prestress to the high tie after the structure has been fully erected and loaded. The second model approximates the effect of prestressing during construction, reflecting the behaviour described in the construction documentation. Although a full staged construction analysis was not performed, a comparison of the resulting structural responses provides insight into the influence of prestressing sequence.

\subsection{Qualitative interpretation}
The results indicate that the timing of prestressing influences the internal force distribution and deformation characteristics of the roof structure. When prestressing is applied only after full erection, the rafters initially experience larger bending moments and deflections under dead load before the restraining effect of the high tie develops. In contrast, prestressing during construction introduces axial compression into the rafters earlier in the erection sequence, which reduces arch spread and decreases bending moments in the rafters.

\subsection{Implications}
These differences also have implications for design and construction tolerances. The construction-stage prestressing sequence can introduce locked-in stresses and influence the final geometry of the structure. As a result, the structural response predicted by a simplified post-erection prestressing assumption may differ from the behaviour observed in the real structure. Understanding these differences is important when interpreting global analysis results and when defining design forces for critical connections such as the rafter splice.

\section{Deliverable 6: Boundary Conditions for More Detailed Analysis}

For a more detailed FE study, the rafter splice between the tusk rafter and arch rafter should be extracted from the global frame model and analysed as a local submodel. The purpose of the local model would be to resolve the stress distribution in the splice plates, bolts or welds, and the adjacent box and I-section walls, which cannot be captured accurately by the beam idealisation used in the global model.

The local model should include a short length of member on each side of the splice so that the boundary conditions are applied away from the connection itself. This reduces artificial stress concentrations caused by restraining the model too close to the detail. A practical choice would be to include approximately one section depth of the arch rafter and one section depth of the tusk rafter beyond the splice on either side.

The preferred boundary condition is to impose the displacement and rotation components extracted from the global beam model at the two cut faces of the local model. In practice, each cut face can be coupled to a reference node so that the sectional deformation remains compatible with the global model. The six kinematic quantities imposed at each end are the three translations ($u_x$, $u_y$, $u_z$) and the three rotations ($r_x$, $r_y$, $r_z$). This approach is preferable to simply fixing one end, because it reproduces the actual deformation state of the connection and avoids introducing artificial restraint moments.

The corresponding section forces should also be checked against the global model outputs. The main actions expected at the splice are axial force $N$, vertical shear $V$, bending moment $M$, and a smaller torsional component due to the curved roof geometry and eccentricity of the connection detail. If the software or modelling approach makes direct displacement transfer difficult, an acceptable alternative is to apply the equivalent set of sectional resultants at the two cut faces, provided that overall equilibrium is maintained.

To prevent rigid body motion in the local model, one reference node may be lightly stabilised after the global displacements have been assigned. This stabilisation should only remove numerical singularities and must not alter the transferred load path. Contact, bolt preload, or weld representation can then be added depending on the intended level of detail. For the purposes of this coursework, the key requirement is that the splice boundaries are driven by the global model response rather than by arbitrary fixed supports.

\section{Span calculation}
\subsection{Required plastic moment for span \(AB\)}


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{Structural Coursework.png}
    \caption{The three sections of the whole span}
    \label{fig:span_sections}
\end{figure}
Span \(AB = 10\,\text{m}\), with \(P = 1000\,\text{kN}\) acting at midspan.
Let the end rotations be \(\theta\).
Then the deflection at the load point is

\[
\delta = 5\theta
\]
The plastic hinge rotation at the load point is

\[
\theta + \theta = 2\theta
\]
The internal work is therefore

\[
W_i = M_{p,AB}(\theta + 2\theta + \theta)
    = 4M_{p,AB}\theta
\]
The external work is

\[
W_e = 1000(5\theta) = 5000\theta
\]
Equating external and internal work,

\[
5000\theta = 4M_{p,AB}\theta
\]
Hence,

\[
M_{p,AB} = 1250\,\text{kNm}
\]


\subsection{Plastic analysis for span \(CD\)}

\subsubsection*{Compatibility at the hinge}


From compatibility,
\[
x\theta = (12-x)\phi
\]
so that
\[
\phi = \frac{x}{12-x}\theta
\]

\subsubsection*{External work}
The deflected shape is triangular on each side, so

\[
W_e = w \times \text{area under deflection shape}
\]
Thus,

\[
W_e = 100\left(\frac{1}{2}\cdot 12 \cdot x\theta\right)
    = 600x\theta
\]
\subsubsection*{Internal work}
At \(C\), the hinge rotation is

\[
\theta
\]
At the positive hinge, the rotation is

\[
\theta + \phi
= \theta + \frac{x}{12-x}\theta
= \theta\left(\frac{12}{12-x}\right)
\]
Hence,

\[
W_i
= M_{p,CD}\left[\theta + \theta\left(\frac{12}{12-x}\right)\right]
= M_{p,CD}\theta\left(\frac{24-x}{12-x}\right)
\]
Equating external and internal work gives

\[
600x\theta
=
M_{p,CD}\theta\left(\frac{24-x}{12-x}\right)
\]
Therefore,

\[
M_{p,CD}
=
600x\left(\frac{12-x}{24-x}\right)
\]


\subsubsection*{Critical mechanism}
To obtain the critical mechanism, maximize \(M_{p,CD}\) with respect to \(x\):

\[
\frac{dM_{p,CD}}{dx} = 0
\]
which gives

\[
x = 12(2-\sqrt{2}) = 7.03\,\text{m}
\]
Hence,

\[
M_{p,CD}
=
600(7.03)\left(\frac{12-7.03}{24-7.03}\right)
\]
Therefore,

\[
M_{p,CD} \approx 1235\,\text{kNm}
\]

\subsection{Plastic analysis of span \(BC\)}
Assume that the end plastic moments are known as
\[
M_{AB}=1250\,\text{kNm}, 
\qquad
M_{CD}=1235\,\text{kNm}
\]
and that an additional plastic hinge forms within span \(BC\) under the applied load \(P=640\,\text{kN}\).
The span length is
\[
BC = 4+6 = 10\,\text{m}
\]
with the point load located \(4\,\text{m}\) from support \(B\) and \(6\,\text{m}\) from support \(C\).

\subsubsection*{Collapse mechanism}
Let the rotation at end \(B\) be \(\theta_B\), and the rotation at end \(C\) be \(\theta_C\).
Since the vertical displacement of the load point must be the same whether measured from the left or the right segment,

\[
4\theta_B = 6\theta_C
\]
Hence,
\[
\theta_C = \frac{4}{6}\theta_B = \frac{2}{3}\theta_B
\]
The vertical displacement of the load point is therefore

\[
\delta = 4\theta_B
\]

\subsubsection*{External work}
The external work done by the applied load is

\[
W_e = P\delta = 640(4\theta_B)=2560\theta_B
\]

\subsubsection*{Internal work}
Internal work is done at the three plastic hinges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item at \(B\): rotation \(=\theta_B\), moment \(=M_{AB}\)
    \item at the internal hinge in span \(BC\): rotation \(=\theta_B+\theta_C\), moment \(=M_{BC}\)
    \item at \(C\): rotation \(=\theta_C\), moment \(=M_{CD}\)
\end{itemize}
Thus,
\[
W_i = M_{AB}\theta_B + M_{BC}(\theta_B+\theta_C) + M_{CD}\theta_C
\]
Substituting the known values and \(\theta_C=\frac{2}{3}\theta_B\),
\[
W_i
=
1250\theta_B
+
M_{BC}\left(\theta_B+\frac{2}{3}\theta_B\right)
+
1235\left(\frac{2}{3}\theta_B\right)
\]
\[
W_i
=
1250\theta_B
+
\frac{5}{3}M_{BC}\theta_B
+
823.33\theta_B
\]

\[
W_i
=
\left(2073.33 + \frac{5}{3}M_{BC}\right)\theta_B
\]

\subsubsection*{Equating external and internal work}
At collapse,

\[
W_e=W_i
\]
so

\[
2560\theta_B
=
\left(2073.33 + \frac{5}{3}M_{BC}\right)\theta_B
\]
Cancelling \(\theta_B\),

\[
2560 = 2073.33 + \frac{5}{3}M_{BC}
\]

\[
\frac{5}{3}M_{BC} = 486.67
\]

\[
M_{BC} = 486.67 \times \frac{3}{5}
\]

\[
M_{BC} \approx 292\,\text{kNm}
\]

\subsubsection*{Result}

Therefore, the required plastic moment capacity of the internal hinge in span \(BC\) is

\[
{M_{BC} \approx 292\,\text{kNm}}
\]
Based on the above result, the plastic moment required at the assumed internal hinge in span \(BC\) is lower than the corresponding plastic moment capacities associated with the end sections. If a single fully plastic moment capacity \(M_p\) is assumed for both sagging and hogging bending, this implies that the section in span \(BC\) would reach the fully plastic state earlier than the end sections \(B\) and \(C\). Consequently, the assumed three-hinge collapse mechanism is not kinematically admissible as the governing failure mechanism for the beam, because the plastic hinge in span \(BC\) would form before the other required hinges had fully developed. In other words, under the assumption that the positive and negative plastic moment capacities are equal, span \(BC\) would not sustain the assumed failure mechanism as drawn; instead, yielding would initiate there prematurely and the actual collapse mechanism would need to be reassessed on the basis of the weaker section controlling the plastic redistribution process.

Assuming grade S355 steel with $f_y = 355 \, \text{MPa}$, the beam is designed based on the governing plastic moment capacity along span AB, where $M_{p,AB} = 1250 \, \text{kNm}$, which is adopted consistently throughout the beam in Figure \ref{fig:line_sketch}.\\

The required plastic section modulus is obtained from:
\[
W_{pl,req} = \frac{M_p \, \gamma_0}{f_y}
\]

Assuming $\gamma_0 = 1.0$,
\[
W_{pl,req} = \frac{1.25 \times 10^9 \times 1.0}{355}
\]

\[
W_{pl,req} = 3.521 \times 10^6 \, \text{mm}^3
\]
\\

Referring to BS EN 1993-1-1:2005 and BS 4-1:2005 section property tables, a UB 610×229×125 section (Figure \ref{fig:UB_610×229×125}) with $W_{pl}=3680\times 10^6 \text{mm}^3$ is adopted for the full beam.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{610x229x125.png}
    \caption{The selected UB 610×229×125 section \cite{sections_ub610x229x125}} 
    \label{fig:UB_610×229×125}
\end{figure}

A uniform section is selected rather than reducing the section in span BC and introducing transitions, as discrete section changes and associated connections can introduce local deviations from idealised beam behaviour and reduce the reliability of stiffness and plastic capacity distribution \cite{hemstad_cantilever_beam_framing}. Maintaining a constant section ensures a more predictable global response and a clearly defined plastic collapse mechanism.

Furthermore, adopting a single rolled section removes the need for moment-resisting splices or transition detailing, simplifying fabrication and erection. Since fabrication and installation typically account for a significantly larger proportion of total project cost than material alone \cite{structural_steel_cost_estimation}, this improves construction efficiency and offsets any marginal material over-design in span BC.

\section{Deliverable 1: Geometry}
The roof geometry was reconstructed from the supplementary drawing using a one-to-one SketchUp model and then transferred into GSA. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:geometry_overview}(a), points labelled \textit{R} define the roof profile and represent the mid-points of the roof section, while points labelled \textit{S} define the support system and lower restraint points. 

The second diagram shows the FE idealisation adopted for analysis. In Figure~\ref{fig:geometry_overview}(b), green denotes beam elements used for the roof profile, red denotes the high tie modelled as a tie element, and blue denotes the support-tree members modelled as bar elements. In GSA, beam elements model axial, bending and torsional effects, bar elements provide axial stiffness only, and tie elements are axial-only elements with zero stiffness in compression. This makes beams suitable for the roof rafters, bars suitable for the tree members, and a tie suitable for the prestressed high tie. Both end supports were modelled as fixed supports, while the pin-connected members were treated consistently on both sides because the connected elements were assumed not to transfer bending moment. This idealisation captures the main load path and preserves the key dimensions required for later loading, validation and mesh sensitivity studies.

Because both the arch rafter and tusk rafter vary in depth along their length, the roof-profile members were implemented in GSA using tapered 1D beam elements rather than prismatic sections. Oasys GSA states that a 1D element can taper between end 1 and end 2 when it is assigned a tapering section profile with two section sizes, and that when a tapering member is divided into multiple elements a taper offset is used so that each element retains the correct local dimensions from its position along the original member \cite{gsa_tapered_elements}. This is important for the present roof model because the varying rafter depth changes the member bending stiffness along the span, so representing that taper improves the fidelity of the global frame idealisation.

\section{Deliverable 2: Material, Section, and Loading Data }
\subsection{Material and Section Properties and Loading Data}

The global FE model was defined using linear-elastic steel. In accordance with the brief, all members were assigned an elastic modulus of \SI{200}{\giga\pascal}, a Poisson's ratio of 0.30 and density of \SI{7850}{\kilogram\per\metre\cubed} were adopted so that self-weight could be included automatically in GSA. S235 steel was adopted for the primary roof members because its elastic modulus complies with the brief requirement, while other material parameters such as shear modulus and strength are not governing in this linear-elastic global analysis. The twin high-tie strands were modelled as a single prestressed tie element with the same total cross-sectional area and an initial prestress of \SI{450}{\mega\pascal}.

Only vertical symmetric loading was considered. The characteristic permanent roof load was taken as the sum of the aluminium standing seam roof (\SI{0.10}{\kilo\pascal}), structural deck (\SI{0.15}{\kilo\pascal}), insulation/acoustic layers (\SI{0.40}{\kilo\pascal}) and the service allowance (\SI{0.15}{\kilo\pascal}), giving $G_k = \SI{0.80}{\kilo\pascal}$. The live load imposed on the roof was taken as $Q_k = \SI{0.60}{\kilo\pascal}$. The governing vertical ultimate limit state (ULS) combination used in the model was
\begin{equation}
1.35G_k + 1.5Q_k
\label{eq:uls_vertical_combination}
\end{equation}
Here, $G_k$ and $Q_k$ were converted to line loads using the projected tributary width carried by one transverse frame. The supplementary roof information states that the main rafters and associated tie-rods are spaced at \SI{18}{\metre} centres, so a tributary width of \SI{18}{\metre} was adopted for the 2D frame idealisation. On this basis, the superimposed permanent roof load is \SI{14.4}{\kilo\newton\per\metre} $(0.80 \times 18)$ and the imposed live load is \SI{10.8}{\kilo\newton\per\metre} $(0.60 \times 18)$, with member self-weight still included separately through the material density. This projected-area treatment is consistent with common roof-loading practice and with the basis commonly used for roof snow loading in Eurocode 1 \cite{bsen_snow}.

The strap members were modelled as bar elements. Therefore, only their axial stiffness was represented in the global model, using an equivalent steel area of $A = 2 \times 500 \times 40 = \SI{40000}{\milli\metre\squared}$. The spacing between the two plates was neglected in this idealisation because the bar elements do not carry bending.

\section{Deliverable 3: Initial Model Validation}
\subsection{Assumptions}

The validation assumes a 2D in-plane frame subjected to vertical symmetric loading. Out-of-plane bending, torsion, adjacent-frame load sharing and detailed connection stiffness are neglected, so the check is intended only to confirm that the global model gives a realistic level of response rather than an exact local solution. This 3D-to-2D simplification is most reasonable near the middle of the roof, where out-of-plane bending moments are expected to remain limited under symmetric loading, but it may be less accurate near the roof edges where edge effects can make out-of-plane bending more significant. Torsion is expected to be a secondary effect because both the applied loading and the main structural arrangement are predominantly symmetric. Lateral buckling and shear buckling are also not represented in the present global model, so these stability modes would need to be checked separately in a more detailed assessment.


\subsection{Model Validation}
As the prestressed arch-and-tie behaviour is difficult to analyse by simple hand calculations, the initial validation was carried out on the non-prestressed model and focused on the bending moment at the tusk-to-arch connection. The loaded tusk was idealised as a \SI{25}{\metre} cantilever under the equivalent ULS line load $w = 1.35 \times 14.4 + 1.5 \times 10.8 = \SI{35.64}{\kilo\newton\per\metre}$, giving a tusk-to-arch connection moment $M = wL^2/2 \approx \SI{11125}{\kilo\newton\metre}$, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:validation_handcalc}. The corresponding FE result is about \SI{16490}{\kilo\newton\metre}, so the model predicts the correct order of magnitude for this key bending action. The difference is expected because the hand check neglects the stiffness of the curved arch, frame interaction between both sides of the roof, and the more complex redistribution captured by the FE model.

An additional source of modelling error was observed at the high-tie connection. The FE model predicts a relatively large vertical deformation there, even though the vertical nodal force associated with the high tie is small compared with the forces transferred by the surrounding members. Using the largest adjacent vertical force from the connection check, the high-tie vertical force is only about $114.6/5901 \approx 1.9\%$ of the neighbouring member force. This suggests that the large vertical movement is more likely to arise from compatibility within the simplified 2D global model and the axial-only idealisation of the tie than from a large vertical load carried by the high tie itself.

\subsection{Model Adequacy}
Basic verification checks were also completed: the GSA solver ran without instability warnings, the vertical reactions balanced the applied gravity loads, and the reconstructed model matched the original drawing on both sides with an overall geometric deviation of approximately 0.5\%. Taken together, the modelling assumptions, validation considerations and verification checks indicate that the global frame model is adequate for an initial study of the roof under symmetric vertical loading. However, it should still be understood as a simplified global model rather than a full representation of the three-dimensional structural behaviour.

Although Mesh 2 is already adequate for deflection and tie force, only Mesh 3 captures the arch curvature accurately enough to stabilise the central bending moment, which is an important response quantity for interpreting the behaviour of the roof frame.

\subsection{Discussion of Results}
Table \ref{tab:prestress_comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:prestress_response} show that prestress changes the global response markedly. Without prestress, the central arch sags under gravity and the two tusks rotate upward to maintain continuity at the arch-tusk junctions. With prestress, the central arch retains a clearer curved profile, so the deformation pattern reverses and the tusks are pushed downward. When the arch sags more, the roof behaves in a more flexural manner and the moment distribution can change sign over parts of the frame, whereas the prestressed case remains closer to arch action.

\subsection{Implications for Design and Tolerance}
These results show that prestress is critical to design interpretation and tolerance assessment. If prestress is neglected, the model overestimates deflection and rafter bending but underestimates the tensile demand in the high tie and its anchorage. This is consistent with the supplementary construction sequence, where staged stressing was used to control geometry and force state, so the prestressed model is adopted as the minimum realistic representation of the completed roof. As shown in Table \ref{tab:mesh_sensitivity}, the change with prestress is significant would imply that without applied prestres, the arch would not be within construction tolerance. Therefore the arch should be installed after prestress is applied.

\section{Deliverable 6: Boundary Conditions for More Detailed Analysis}

For a more detailed FE study, the rafter splice between the tusk rafter and arch rafter should be extracted from the global frame model and analysed as a local submodel. The purpose of the local model would be to resolve the stress distribution in the splice plates, bolts or welds, and the adjacent box and I-section walls, which cannot be captured accurately by the beam idealisation used in the global model.

The local model should include a short length of member on each side of the splice so that the boundary conditions are applied away from the connection itself. This reduces artificial stress concentrations caused by restraining the model too close to the detail. A practical choice would be to include approximately one section depth of the arch rafter and one section depth of the tusk rafter beyond the splice on either side.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{bc_connection.png}
\caption{Recommended boundary conditions for the local splice model}
\label{fig:bc_connection}
\end{figure}

The preferred boundary condition is to impose the displacement and rotation components extracted from the global beam model at the two cut faces of the local model. In practice, each cut face can be coupled to a reference node so that the sectional deformation remains compatible with the global model. The six kinematic quantities imposed at each end are the three translations ($u_x$, $u_y$, $u_z$) and the three rotations ($r_x$, $r_y$, $r_z$). This approach is preferable to simply fixing one end, because it reproduces the actual deformation state of the connection and avoids introducing artificial restraint moments.

The corresponding section forces should also be checked against the global model outputs. The main actions expected at the splice are axial force $N$, vertical shear $V$, bending moment $M$, and a smaller torsional component due to the curved roof geometry and eccentricity of the connection detail. If the software or modelling approach makes direct displacement transfer difficult, an acceptable alternative is to apply the equivalent set of sectional resultants at the two cut faces, provided that overall equilibrium is maintained.

To prevent rigid body motion in the local model, one reference node may be lightly stabilised after the global displacements have been assigned. This stabilisation should only remove numerical singularities and must not alter the transferred load path. Contact, bolt preload, or weld representation can then be added depending on the intended level of detail. For the purposes of this coursework, the key requirement is that the splice boundaries are driven by the global model response rather than by arbitrary fixed supports.

Section cuts are located approximately  one local section depth  away from the splice connection along the axis of the member. This avoids local stress disturbance. As the depth varies along the rafter, the local section depth is equal to the splice-region depth (3.6 m), cuts A–A and B–B are placed approximately 3.6 m from the splice on either side. At both cut faces, the translational and rotational displacements extracted from the global FE model are imposed. This ensures compatibility between the local splice model and the global structural response.
